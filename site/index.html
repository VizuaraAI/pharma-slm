Everything was built from scratch.

A real language model is six honest steps. We did each one ourselves — no shortcuts, no borrowed weights. This is the same recipe behind GPT-style models, shrunk to something we can train and explain end-to-end.

Why custom data?

To learn English and pharma, we blended ~50% general text with ~50% biomedical. Too much domain and it overfits; too little and it doesn't know the field. We measured the sweet spot — it's not a guess.

Why a custom tokenizer?

Off-the-shelf tokenizers shatter medical words. Ours hits a fertility 1.57 on pharma text — fewer tokens per term, so the model spends its capacity on meaning, not spelling.

Why from scratch?

So every number is ours and explainable — and so we could train the tokenizer on day one. It's the path the best small biomedical models (BioGPT, PubMedBERT) actually took.

03

The surprise: more pharma data isn't always better.

The model trained on 75% pharma got the best "study score" (lowest training loss) — but the worst real exam result. It had memorized the style of medical papers without learning to reason. Lower loss didn't mean better answers. The balanced model won. That's why we measure on real questions, and it's the core lesson of the whole project.

⚖️

Balanced (the winner)

50% pharma / 50% English. Learns the field and stays a fluent writer. Generalizes to questions it never saw.

📉

Pharma-overloaded

75% pharma. Best on paper, brittle in practice — it over-rehearsed a small corpus and lost the plot on real Q&A.

06

So what does “the exam score is capacity-bound at 0.29” mean?

This is the one sentence to remember — here it is in plain English.

The model scores ~29% on 4-option medical exams. Pure guessing (the grey line) is 25%. So on hard exam questions it's barely above a coin-toss-style guess.

✅

What it's genuinely good at

Writing fluent, mostly-accurate pharma explanations — drug uses, side effects, mechanisms. Generating good text needs far less reasoning horsepower. You saw this in the chat above.

🎲

What it struggles with

Trick multiple-choice questions that need multi-step clinical reasoning. It answers, but only a little better than guessing — and unreliably (it flips on questions it "should" know).

“Capacity-bound” = the model is simply too small (350M parameters) to do the multi-step reasoning hard exams demand. We proved it: training 5× longer on 3× more data did not move the exam score — it stayed near guessing. The bottleneck is brain size, not study time.

The student analogy

It's read every textbook (great recall, fluent writing) but can't hold a tricky multi-step problem in its head. More reading won't fix that — it needs a bigger head.

What it means for you

You have a useful pharma writing & explanation assistant — not a board-exam solver. For drafting, summarizing, and explaining, it works today.

How to make it pass exams

Scale the model to ~1B+ parameters. The entire pipeline already supports it — change two numbers in a config and re-run. That's the next experiment.

Honest note: on aggregate (1,000 questions) MedMCQA improved 0.29→0.32; on any single trick question it's still a coin-flip. The capacity wall lifts at 1.3B, it doesn't vanish — for board-exam accuracy you keep going (2.7B+). Cost of the 1.3B run: ~$880; one CUDA-OOM caught & fixed (small micro-batch + grad-accum), and the learning rate had to drop from 1e-3 → 5e-4 (found by a quick ablation at the new size).

09

RAG: answers it can actually cite.

A small model's biggest weakness is confidently-wrong facts. The fix isn't a bigger model — it's retrieval: pull real PubMed passages at question time and have the 1.3B answer grounded in them, with citations. We indexed 300,000 PubMed passages (PubMedBERT embeddings + FAISS). Now every answer shows its sources, so you can verify it.

The tradeoff (honestly): retrieval quality matters — a vague query can pull tangential papers — but grounding + citations is the highest-ROI reliability layer for a domain assistant, far cheaper than scaling. The live chat above is now the 1.3B; RAG adds the sources.

10

How an enterprise builds an LLM — the playbook.

The exact roadmap we followed, generalized. Purple steps are decisions; each step lists the ablations to run and a rough cost (at the 350M scale). The single most important rule is at step 3: judge models on real tasks, not on training loss.

0

Decide scope & budget

Pick the task first — it sets everything. Generation/summarization → a small model is fine. Multi-step reasoning / exams → you need ~1B+. Then choose from-scratch (full control + custom tokenizer, needs lots of domain data) vs continued-pretraining (faster, data-light, inherits reasoning).

Decision: size ↔ task ↔ budgetCost: planning only
1

Gather data + build a custom tokenizer

Blend domain text with general English. Watch the domain-data ceiling (we hit a 1B-token wall and had to expand). Train a custom BPE — it's free when from-scratch and the one thing you can't do later.

Ablate later: mix ratioCost: ~$5–10 (CPU)
2

Build the spine — then smoke-test tiny

Model + DDP trainer + eval harness + infra. Always run a tiny end-to-end test first (it caught 4 real bugs for us for ~$2 before any big spend).

Cost: ~$2
3

Ablation sweeps — parallel & cheap

Short runs, one GPU each, that self-abandon if they stall. Round 1: learning rate. Round 2: data mix — judge on the downstream task, not loss (our best-loss model gave the worst answers). Round 3: architecture (e.g. GQA).

Ablate: LR ×4 · mix ×3 · archGolden rule: tasks > lossCost: ~$15–30
4

Production fleet — full runs in parallel

Train a few full candidates that vary your biggest unknown (for us: the general↔domain mix), all at once, and pick the winner by evaluation. Watch for overfitting (our 75%-domain run overfit).

Ablate: the one big unknownCost: ~$150–300
5

Scale the winner — train it long

Over-train past Chinchilla (small models keep improving). Make it resume-capable. If the domain corpus is thin, expand the data before adding tokens, or you'll just memorize.

Cost: ~$200 (350M) → scales with size
6

Instruction-tune (SFT)

Pre-training gives knowledge; SFT gives behavior (it answers instead of rambling). Cheap, huge UX impact. Loss-mask to the answer tokens only.

Cost: ~$20
7

Evaluate, deploy & demo

Grade on held-out real tasks, ship a live endpoint, and let people try it. If facts matter more than scale lets you afford, add retrieval (RAG) — it boosts accuracy cheaply without a bigger model.

Cost: ~$1 / serving session

Golden rules we learned the hard way

Judge on tasks, not loss

Our lowest-loss model gave the worst answers. Always score on the real downstream task.

Match size to the task

350M writes well but can't reason through exams. Capability is a function of scale, not just data.

Over-train small models

Push well past Chinchilla-optimal — that's what turns "grammatical" into "fluent."

Experiment in parallel, kill losers

Run a fleet at once and self-abandon the bad runs. Don't tune one model at a time.

Make long jobs durable

Detach from your laptop, checkpoint often, resume on retry. We lost a model to a non-detached run.

Smoke-test before you spend

A $2 tiny run catches the bugs that would waste a $200 one.
